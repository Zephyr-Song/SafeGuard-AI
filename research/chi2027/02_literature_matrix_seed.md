## Theme 1: Controllable LLM Agents and Cognitive Bias

### CoBRA: Cognitive Bias Regulator for Social Agents

- Source: https://github.com/AISmithLab/CoBRA
- Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.13588
- Relevance: Primary inspiration.
- Core idea: Natural-language personas are insufficient for controlling agent behavior; bias should be measured and controlled through an index and regulation engine.
- Gap for us: CoBRA focuses on agent bias control, not how researchers use synthetic stakeholders to prepare ethical action research.

### Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior

- Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442
- Relevance: Foundational work on believable LLM agents and social simulation.
- Core idea: LLM agents can produce believable individual and social behaviors in simulated environments.
- Gap for us: Believability is not enough for high-risk research preparation; researchers also need controllability, limitation awareness, and ethics scaffolding.

## Theme 2: Synthetic Users and Risks of Replacing Participants

### LLMs Replacing Human Participants Can Misportray Identity Groups

- Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.01908
- Relevance: Important cautionary foundation.
- Core idea: LLM-simulated participants can flatten, stereotype, or misrepresent identity groups.
- Gap for us: Rather than replacing participants, SafeBARS uses synthetic stakeholders only as pre-fieldwork rehearsal aids.

### Papers to Add

- Synthetic users in UX research.
- LLMs as survey respondents.
- AI-assisted qualitative research.
- Risks of synthetic data in HCI research.

## Theme 3: Participatory Design, Action Research, and Reflexivity

### Papers to Add

- Participatory action research in HCI.
- Reflexivity in HCI and CSCW fieldwork.
- Ethical engagement with vulnerable communities.
- Design justice and community accountability.
- Pre-fieldwork preparation and researcher positionality.

Working gap:

Existing participatory and action research literature emphasizes reflexivity, situated engagement, and accountability. However, there is limited work on how AI systems can scaffold pre-fieldwork rehearsal without replacing real community participation.

## Theme 4: Digital Safety and Online Fraud

### ROLESafe: Designing Role-Based Simulation to Support Older Adults' Online Fraud Awareness

- Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.12324
- Relevance: Close related work and a reason not to frame SafeBARS as only anti-fraud training for users.
- Core idea: Role-based simulation can support older adults' online fraud awareness.
- Gap for us: ROLESafe targets end-user education. SafeBARS targets researcher preparation and action research scaffolding.

### Papers to Add

- Anti-phishing education in HCI.
- Online fraud prevention and older adults.
- Digital safety literacy.
- Trauma-informed safety education.
- Scam reporting and help-seeking behavior.

## Theme 5: Responsible AI, Trust Calibration, and Human-AI Interaction

### Papers to Add

- Trust calibration in AI-assisted decision making.
- AI explanations and transparency dashboards.
- Human-AI co-creation in research and design.
- Responsible use of AI in qualitative inquiry.
- AI limitation communication.

Working gap:

SafeBARS needs to make limitations visible so researchers do not over-trust synthetic stakeholders or treat them as evidence about real communities.
