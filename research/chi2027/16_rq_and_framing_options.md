# Research Question and Framing Options

Last updated: 2026-06-16

## Purpose

This memo gives three possible framings for SafeBARS. Each option could become a CHI paper, but they emphasize different contributions.

## Option A: Researcher Reflexivity Framing

### One-Sentence Framing

SafeBARS helps researchers use bias-calibrated synthetic stakeholders to surface assumptions, missing perspectives, and ethical risks before high-risk fieldwork.

### Primary Research Question

How can bias-calibrated synthetic stakeholders scaffold researcher reflexivity during the planning of high-risk action research?

### Secondary Research Questions

RQ2. What kinds of hidden assumptions and missing stakeholders do researchers identify through synthetic stakeholder rehearsal?

RQ3. How do researchers revise research plans after interacting with bias-calibrated stakeholder profiles?

### Main Contribution

An AI-assisted reflexivity scaffold for HCI/action research preparation.

### Best Evidence

- Pre/post research plan comparison.
- Interview quotes showing changed assumptions.
- Thematic analysis of reflection reports.

### Strengths

- Very CHI/HCI.
- Strong ethical stance.
- Does not require large quantitative study.

### Risks

- Could be criticized as too qualitative or too obvious unless the findings are rich.

### Best Paper Title Variant

Rehearsing Before the Field: Bias-Calibrated Synthetic Stakeholders for Reflexive Action Research Planning

## Option B: Trust Calibration Framing

### One-Sentence Framing

SafeBARS studies how explicit bias profiles and limitation-aware dashboards shape researchers' trust in synthetic stakeholders.

### Primary Research Question

How do bias-profile controls and limitation-aware reflection dashboards affect researchers' trust calibration when using synthetic stakeholders for study planning?

### Secondary Research Questions

RQ2. When do researchers find synthetic stakeholder outputs useful, misleading, or insufficient?

RQ3. What interface features help researchers treat synthetic stakeholders as rehearsal aids rather than participant replacements?

### Main Contribution

Design knowledge for trust-calibrated AI tools in research planning.

### Best Evidence

- Comparison between ordinary LLM role-play and SafeBARS.
- Trust calibration questionnaire.
- Interview analysis on overreliance and skepticism.

### Strengths

- Strong responsible AI angle.
- Directly addresses the concern that synthetic stakeholders could be harmful.
- Easier to connect to HAI literature.

### Risks

- Could drift away from action research if not grounded in concrete study planning tasks.

### Best Paper Title Variant

Not a Participant, Not a Proxy: Calibrating Trust in Synthetic Stakeholders for High-Risk Research Planning

## Option C: Controllability and Rehearsal Framing

### One-Sentence Framing

SafeBARS adapts CoBRA's bias-control insight into a rehearsal system where stakeholder risk-response profiles can be adjusted to stress-test research plans.

### Primary Research Question

How can controllable stakeholder risk-response profiles support pre-fieldwork rehearsal for high-risk digital safety research?

### Secondary Research Questions

RQ2. Can prompt-level risk-response profiles produce useful and interpretable differences in synthetic stakeholder behavior?

RQ3. How do researchers use controlled variation across stakeholder profiles to improve their study designs?

### Main Contribution

A controllable synthetic stakeholder system and early evidence of its usefulness in research rehearsal.

### Best Evidence

- Small monotonicity/consistency test of stakeholder profiles.
- Formative study with researchers.
- Examples of plan improvements across profile variations.

### Strengths

- Closest to CoBRA.
- Strong technical-system narrative.
- Easier to explain why bias profiles matter.

### Risks

- Reviewers may expect stronger technical validation.
- Could be seen as weaker than CoBRA if we overemphasize control.

### Best Paper Title Variant

From Bias Control to Research Rehearsal: Risk-Calibrated Synthetic Stakeholders for Digital Safety Action Research

## Recommendation

The strongest CHI framing is Option A with elements of Option B.

Recommended primary framing:

SafeBARS is a reflexive pre-fieldwork rehearsal scaffold.

Recommended supporting angle:

The system is designed for trust calibration, explicitly preventing synthetic stakeholders from being treated as participant replacements.

Recommended technical connection:

Bias profiles are inspired by CoBRA and used as controllable rehearsal variables.

## Recommended RQ Set

RQ1. How can bias-calibrated synthetic stakeholders scaffold researchers' preparation for high-risk action research?

RQ2. What assumptions, missing stakeholders, ethical risks, and safety gaps do researchers surface through SafeBARS rehearsal?

RQ3. How do explicit bias profiles and limitation-aware reflection reports shape researchers' trust calibration toward synthetic stakeholder outputs?

## Recommended Contribution Set

1. A problem framing for safe synthetic stakeholder rehearsal in high-risk action research.
2. SafeBARS, a researcher-facing system that combines stakeholder role coverage, risk-response profiles, rehearsal interaction, and reflection dashboards.
3. Empirical findings from formative/pilot study showing how researchers revise plans and calibrate trust.
4. Design implications for AI research tools that support, rather than replace, participatory engagement.

## Supervisor Discussion Questions

Ask the supervisor:

1. Should the paper emphasize reflexivity, trust calibration, or controllable stakeholder behavior?
2. Should online fraud remain the main domain, or should we frame it as one example of high-risk digital safety?
3. Is a formative study enough, or should we plan a small comparison pilot?
4. Which participant group is easiest to recruit quickly: HCI students, UX researchers, AI safety researchers, or mixed early-career researchers?
5. Should the system be evaluated as a design probe, a prototype tool, or a method scaffold?
