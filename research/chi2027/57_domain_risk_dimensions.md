# Domain Risk Dimensions for SafeBARS

Last updated: 2026-06-19

Purpose: extract online-fraud and older-adult risk dimensions that SafeBARS should support in prompts, stakeholder profiles, reflection reports, and study coding.

## Why Online Fraud Is a Good Test Context

Online fraud prevention is not only a digital-literacy problem. It involves money, shame, trust, family relationships, institutional response, and fear of losing autonomy. This makes it a strong context for testing pre-fieldwork rehearsal because a poorly worded research plan can easily create emotional or ethical harm.

## Core Risk Dimensions

### 1. Shame and Embarrassment

Researchers may want to ask about financial loss, why someone trusted a scammer, or what warning signs they missed. These questions can sound blaming even when the researcher intends to learn.

SafeBARS should surface:

- discomfort with disclosure;
- fear of being judged;
- embarrassment about loss or trust;
- requests to reframe questions around learning, support, and prevention.

Example safer revision:

> Instead of "Why did you believe the message?", ask "What made the message seem trustworthy at the time, and what support would have helped you check it?"

### 2. Privacy and Data Boundaries

Fraud stories can contain financial details, family conflict, platform names, phone numbers, screenshots, and reporting histories.

SafeBARS should surface:

- who can access participant answers;
- whether recordings are needed;
- how identifiable details will be removed;
- whether participants can decline specific disclosure.

Example safer revision:

> We will not ask for exact loss amounts unless participants choose to share them. We will explain why each detail is requested and how it will be anonymized.

### 3. Family Support and Family Pressure

Family can be protective, but family involvement can also create conflict, shame, or loss of autonomy.

SafeBARS should surface:

- younger family members as helpers;
- older adults refusing help;
- tension between protection and independence;
- risks of inviting family members into research without consent boundaries.

Example stakeholder profile:

> Family helper who wants to protect an older adult but may speak over them or pressure them.

### 4. Institutional Distrust

Participants may distrust universities, banks, police, platforms, or community organizations, especially when money loss or fraud reporting is involved.

SafeBARS should surface:

- why the researcher needs the data;
- whether answers could affect services or reporting;
- whether institutional affiliation makes participants cautious;
- what community partner can help build trust.

Example safer revision:

> Clarify that participation is voluntary, unrelated to services or reporting outcomes, and that participants can skip questions about institutions.

### 5. Distress and Emotional Safety

Fraud experiences can involve loss, guilt, family conflict, or fear. A workshop can also trigger distress by showing realistic scam examples.

SafeBARS should surface:

- need for pause and skip options;
- distress protocol;
- support resources;
- lower-intensity alternatives to detailed storytelling.

Example safer revision:

> Add a script that participants may pause, skip, or stop at any time, and provide follow-up support resources.

### 6. Participant Burden

A two-hour workshop, detailed story sharing, or repeated reflection can be demanding, especially for sensitive topics.

SafeBARS should surface:

- session length;
- cognitive load;
- repeated disclosure;
- whether participants need breaks or shorter formats.

Example safer revision:

> Split the workshop into shorter modules and make personal story sharing optional.

### 7. Deficit Framing

Fraud prevention research can easily imply that older adults are careless, gullible, or technologically incompetent.

SafeBARS should surface:

- wording that blames participants;
- assumptions that education alone solves the problem;
- missing structural factors such as platform design, scammer sophistication, and reporting support.

Example safer revision:

> Frame scams as adversarial and socially engineered rather than as participant mistakes.

### 8. Missing Stakeholders

Fraud prevention involves more than the target participant.

SafeBARS should prompt consideration of:

- older adults;
- family helpers;
- community workers;
- banks;
- platforms;
- police/reporting agencies;
- peer support groups.

Example safer revision:

> Add a stakeholder map before recruitment and ask a community partner to review the plan.

## How These Dimensions Should Appear in the Prototype

### In stakeholder prompts

Stakeholders should push back when a plan asks for exact loss, demands personal detail, assumes trust in institutions, or frames fraud as a personal mistake.

### In provider comparison

Provider comparison should show which models identify privacy, shame, consent, burden, and trust concerns.

### In reflection reports

Reflection should convert stakeholder objections into actionable revisions:

- revise wording;
- clarify consent;
- add data boundaries;
- add skip/pause options;
- shorten session;
- include missing stakeholders;
- verify assumptions with community partners.

### In study coding

When analyzing pre/post plans, code whether participants add or improve:

- privacy language;
- consent/withdrawal language;
- distress protocol;
- non-blaming wording;
- stakeholder map;
- participant-burden reduction;
- community verification step.
