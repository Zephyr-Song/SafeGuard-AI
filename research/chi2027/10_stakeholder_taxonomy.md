# Stakeholder Taxonomy for SafeBARS v1

Last updated: 2026-06-16

## Purpose

This taxonomy defines the first stakeholder roles for SafeBARS. The goal is to help researchers rehearse action research plans from multiple perspectives without claiming that synthetic stakeholders represent real communities.

Each stakeholder role is a rehearsal lens.

## Role Selection Principles

SafeBARS should include stakeholders who differ by:

- Relationship to the harm.
- Power and authority.
- Access to resources.
- Privacy and safety concerns.
- Likely involvement in intervention delivery.
- Potential to be missed by researchers.

## v1 Stakeholder Roles

### 1. Affected Participant

Description:

A person directly affected by the digital safety issue, such as someone who received scam messages, experienced online fraud, or struggled with AI-mediated risk.

Why include:

Researchers often center this person but may frame questions in ways that increase shame, blame, or distress.

Common concerns:

- Privacy.
- Shame.
- Fear of judgment.
- Financial loss.
- Family reaction.
- Trust in authorities.

Useful rehearsal tasks:

- Interview question testing.
- Consent explanation.
- Distress handling.
- Help-seeking discussion.

Recommended bias dimensions:

- shame_stigma_sensitivity
- privacy_sensitivity
- help_seeking_reluctance
- institutional_distrust
- distress_sensitivity

### 2. Family Helper or Caregiver

Description:

A family member, friend, or caregiver who helps the affected participant navigate digital safety decisions.

Why include:

Family helpers often shape reporting, recovery, and future prevention, but may also introduce pressure or blame.

Common concerns:

- Protecting the affected person.
- Anger toward scammers.
- Frustration with participant decisions.
- Desire for practical advice.
- Family privacy.

Useful rehearsal tasks:

- Intervention design.
- Family-centered safety planning.
- Avoiding victim-blaming language.

Recommended bias dimensions:

- authority_trust
- confirmation_bias
- victim_blaming_risk
- urgency_sensitivity
- privacy_sensitivity

### 3. Community Worker

Description:

A local staff member, volunteer, social worker, educator, librarian, or community organizer who supports the target community.

Why include:

Community workers understand practical constraints and can reveal whether a research plan is feasible, burdensome, or misaligned with community routines.

Common concerns:

- Limited time.
- Limited funding.
- Participant burden.
- Trust building.
- Language accessibility.
- Support after study sessions.

Useful rehearsal tasks:

- Recruitment planning.
- Workshop design.
- Safety protocol review.
- Feasibility testing.

Recommended bias dimensions:

- resource_constraint_sensitivity
- participant_burden_sensitivity
- institutional_trust
- practical_feasibility_focus

### 4. Platform or Service Representative

Description:

A representative from a digital platform, bank, telecom service, reporting system, or online safety service.

Why include:

Platform stakeholders shape reporting and prevention, but may prioritize compliance, scale, or liability over lived experience.

Common concerns:

- Policy compliance.
- Data privacy.
- Scalability.
- Liability.
- User education.
- Reporting workflows.

Useful rehearsal tasks:

- Intervention integration.
- Reporting workflow testing.
- Data collection boundary setting.

Recommended bias dimensions:

- institutional_defensiveness
- privacy_sensitivity
- compliance_focus
- scalability_focus

### 5. Authority Figure

Description:

A police officer, government staff member, school official, bank fraud officer, or other authority figure relevant to the research context.

Why include:

Authority figures may increase trust for some participants and fear or distrust for others. They also shape help-seeking pathways.

Common concerns:

- Evidence.
- Procedure.
- Reporting responsibility.
- Fraud prevention.
- Public safety.

Useful rehearsal tasks:

- Authority involvement planning.
- Referral language.
- Trust and fear scenario testing.

Recommended bias dimensions:

- authority_pressure
- institutional_trust
- procedural_focus
- empathy_level

### 6. Skeptical or Disengaged Participant

Description:

A person who is not convinced the research or intervention is useful, relevant, safe, or worth their time.

Why include:

Researchers often design for cooperative participants. Skeptical stakeholders can reveal burdens, mistrust, and weak value propositions.

Common concerns:

- "This is not useful."
- "I do not want to talk about this."
- "Researchers just collect data and leave."
- "I do not trust AI tools."

Useful rehearsal tasks:

- Recruitment message testing.
- Consent and value explanation.
- Trust-building strategy.

Recommended bias dimensions:

- institutional_distrust
- research_fatigue
- privacy_sensitivity
- help_seeking_reluctance

### 7. Peer Observer

Description:

A peer, neighbor, or community member who is not directly affected but observes the issue and may influence social norms.

Why include:

Peer norms can amplify stigma, social proof, reporting behavior, or misinformation.

Common concerns:

- Community reputation.
- Normalization of scams.
- Social embarrassment.
- Informal advice.

Useful rehearsal tasks:

- Social norm intervention.
- Community messaging.
- Misinformation response.

Recommended bias dimensions:

- social_proof_susceptibility
- stigma_sensitivity
- confirmation_bias

## Recommended v1 Default Set

For the first formative study, include four default roles:

1. Affected Participant.
2. Family Helper.
3. Community Worker.
4. Skeptical Participant.

Optional roles:

5. Platform Representative.
6. Authority Figure.

## Role Safety Boundaries

All synthetic stakeholders should include limitation language:

- This is a fictional rehearsal role.
- It does not represent a real person or group.
- It is designed to help inspect assumptions.
- It cannot validate real community needs.
- Real participants and community partners remain necessary.
