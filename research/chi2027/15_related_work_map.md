# Related Work Map

Last updated: 2026-06-16

## Proposed Related Work Structure

The related work should be organized around the argument, not around technology categories.

## Section 1: LLM Agents and the Need for Controllability

Purpose:

Explain why believable synthetic agents are not enough.

Key works:

- Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior.
- CoBRA: Cognitive Bias Regulator for Social Agents.
- Lost in Simulation: LLM-Simulated Users are Unreliable Proxies.

Argument:

LLM agents can produce plausible social behavior, but plausibility does not guarantee control, robustness, or ethical usefulness in high-risk settings. CoBRA shows one way to make behavior measurable and controllable. SafeBARS transfers this insight from agent evaluation to researcher-facing rehearsal.

Transition:

However, controllability alone does not solve the ethical problem of using LLMs in place of real people.

## Section 2: Synthetic Participants and the Risk of Replacement

Purpose:

Explain why SafeBARS rejects the "synthetic participant as evidence" framing.

Key works:

- Large Language Models that Replace Human Participants Can Harmfully Misportray and Flatten Identity Groups.
- Can AI Replace Human Subjects?
- Can Large Language Models Replace Humans in the Systematic Review Process?

Argument:

LLMs may be useful supplements in some research workflows, but replacement is especially risky when identity, positionality, lived experience, and marginalization matter. SafeBARS therefore treats synthetic stakeholders as rehearsal prompts and assumption probes, not empirical substitutes.

Transition:

This boundary aligns with participatory and action research traditions that emphasize doing research with communities rather than on or for them.

## Section 3: Participatory and Action Research as Ethical Grounding

Purpose:

Ground the project in HCI's concern with stakeholder agency, reflexivity, and responsible engagement.

Key works:

- Participatory action research foundations.
- Community-based participatory research foundations.
- The Participatory Turn in AI Design.
- Enabling Value Sensitive AI Systems through Participatory Design Fictions.

Argument:

Participatory and action research emphasize collaboration, situated knowledge, and community agency. AI tools for research preparation must therefore help researchers enter fieldwork more carefully, not shortcut the need for participation.

Transition:

Digital safety domains such as online fraud make this preparation especially important because research can involve shame, harm, vulnerability, family dynamics, and institutional distrust.

## Section 4: Digital Safety and Online Fraud as a High-Risk Test Context

Purpose:

Justify online fraud as the initial context while avoiding over-narrow framing.

Key works:

- ROLESafe.
- Intergenerational Support for Deepfake Scams Targeting Older Adults.
- Sixteen Years of Phishing User Studies.

Argument:

Fraud prevention research is socially important and methodologically challenging. Prior work has developed anti-fraud education and role-based simulations for learners, but SafeBARS targets the researchers preparing such interventions.

Transition:

Because SafeBARS itself is an AI tool used in research planning, it must also support appropriate trust and limitation awareness.

## Section 5: Trust Calibration and Human-AI Research Tools

Purpose:

Explain why trust calibration is a design goal and study outcome.

Key works:

- Guidelines for Human-AI Interaction.
- Calibrated Trust in Dealing with LLM Hallucinations.
- Trust-Calibrated Code Review.

Argument:

Human-AI systems need to help users form appropriate expectations and avoid overreliance. In SafeBARS, trust calibration means researchers understand that synthetic stakeholders can reveal assumptions but cannot validate community needs.

## Final Gap Statement

Existing work shows that LLM agents can be believable, that controllability matters, and that replacing human participants is risky. Participatory research further demands careful engagement with affected communities. Yet there is limited work on how to design AI systems that use synthetic stakeholders safely as bounded rehearsal aids for researchers preparing high-risk action research.

SafeBARS addresses this gap by combining bias-calibrated stakeholder profiles, rehearsal interaction, reflection scaffolding, and trust calibration in a researcher-facing system.

## Suggested Related Work Opening Paragraph

SafeBARS sits at the intersection of controllable LLM agents, synthetic participant critique, participatory action research, and trust-calibrated human-AI interaction. Rather than treating synthetic stakeholders as proxies for real participants, we ask how they might serve as bounded rehearsal aids that help researchers inspect assumptions before fieldwork. This distinction is central: our goal is not to improve the realism of simulated users, but to design an AI scaffold that supports safer and more reflexive preparation for real community engagement.
